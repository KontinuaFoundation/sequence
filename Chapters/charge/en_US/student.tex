If you rub a balloon against your hair, then place it next to a wall, it will stick. Why does this happen? This is exactly what will be explored in this chapter on \textbf{charge} and \textbf{electrostatics}.
This is because the balloon has stripped some electrons from your hair, and now the ballon has slightly
more electrons than protons. We say that it has gotten an \textit{net electrical
	charge}\index{electrical charge}, or an imbalance between protons and electrons. While any \textbf{friction} or contact between two objects can cause a difference in electric charge, \textit{net charge cannot be created or destroyed}.\index{net charge}
In this case, the balloon has a negative electrical charge. Objects with
slightly more protons than electrons have a positive charge. Consequently, your
hair now has a positive charge. Note that the differences are equal but opposite.

An object with a negative charge and an object with a positive charge will be \emph{attracted}\index{attraction}
to each other. Two objects with the same charge will be \emph{repelled}\index{repel}
by each other. This charge originates from a subatomic level, when protons (positively charged) and electrons (negatively charged) are present in an atom at \emph{unequal amounts}.\index{protons}\index{electrons}\index{atoms}

This charge, referred to as \textit{elementary charge}\index{elementary
charge|seealso{coulombs}} is represented by the base unit $e$ (not to be
confused with the $e^{x}$ function in math disciplines). This value, known as the elementary charge ($e$), represents the smallest indivisible unit of electric charge. Much like counting physical coins, you cannot have a "fractional" coin. Similarly, an object's net charge must be an integer multiple of $e$. In physics, we describe this property by saying that charge is quantized ($Q=ne$ where $n$ is an integer).

In this way, we call elementary charge \emph{quantized}\index{quantized}. This was shown back in Because it is quantized, we
represent the charge of a particle as $q$. The SI (International System of Units)
measures charge in \emph{coulombs}\index{coulombs}. The charge of a single
proton is about $1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ coulombs.

Two objects with charges are bound to either attract (pull) or repel (push) on
each other. This is a type of force referred to as \emph{electric force}\index{electric
	force}.

\begin{mdframed}
	[style=important, frametitle={Coulomb's Law}]\index{coulombs!Coulomb's law}

	If two objects with charge $q_{1}$ and $q_{2}$ (in coulombs) are $r$ meters
	from each other, the force of attraction or repulsion (electric force) is given
	by

	\begin{equation}
		F = k\frac{ q_{1}q_{2}}{r^{2}}
		\label{eq:coulomb_law}
	\end{equation}

	% removed abs bc of alignment to ap standards (understanding sign of force)

	where $F$ is in newtons and $K$ is Coulomb's constant: about
	$8.988 \times 10^{9}$. You may use $9 \times 10^{9}\, \mathrm{N}\cdot \mathrm{m}
		^{2}/ \mathrm{C}$ in your calculations.

	This $k$ value comes from $\dfrac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0}$, a \textit{relationship
		with the permittivity of free space}, $\epsilon_{0}$.\footnotemark
	You may see this as $F_{e}$, $F_{q}$, or $F_{c}$, but they all refer to the electric
	force and the standard is $F_{e}$.
\end{mdframed}
\footnotetext{A note on the permittivity of free space: $\epsilon_0$ (or the electric constant) represents the capability of a vacuum to permit electric field lines. Its exact value in SI units is $\epsilon_0 \approx 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \text{ C}^2/(\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2)$. This tells us that the speed of light isn't just a number but rather it is strictly determined by how quickly the universe can allow electric and magnetic fields to change. Using $k$ allows for quick math, especially when fields become spherical and the $\pi$ factor is eliminated easily.}
Note how similar this looks to Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation:
\[
	F_{g}= G \frac{m_{1}m_{2}}{r^{2}}
\]

Both laws follow an inverse-square relationship, meaning the force decreases with
the square of the distance. However, gravity is always attractive, while
electric force can be either attractive or repulsive depending on the signs of
the charges ($q_1$ and $q_2$).

If this force is negative, it represents the idea that the two given particles are
attracted to each other, as this would mean one of the particles must have a
negative charge.

If this force is positive, it would mean there is a push or repelling force on each
of the particles.

What if this force is zero? This would only result if one or both particles have
zero charge. This would mean that they do not have an electric force, $F_{E}= 0$.

\begin{Exercise}
	[title={Coulomb's Law}, label=charged_balloons]

	Two balloons are charged with an identical quantity and type of charge:
	$-5 \times 10^{-9}$ coulombs. They are held apart at a separation distance of 12
	cm. Determine the magnitude of the electrical force of repulsion between them.
\end{Exercise}
\begin{Answer}
	[ref=charged_balloons]
	\[
		F = K\frac{\lvert q_{1}q_{2}\rvert}{r^{2}}= (8.988 \times 10^{9}) \frac{(-5
			\times 10^{-9})(-5 \times 10^{-9})}{0.12^{2}}= \frac{224.7 \times 10^{-9}}{0.0144}
		= 15.6 \times 10^{-6} \,\text{N or} 15.6 \, \text{ micronewtons}
	\]
\end{Answer}


At this point, you might ask ``If the wall has zero charge, why is the balloon attracted
to it?'' The answer: the electrons in the wall move away from the balloon,
polarizing the atoms. The negative charge on the balloon pushes electrons away from
itself, so the surface of the wall gets a mild positive charge. The negative charge
on the balloon is attracted more to the positive charge on the surface of the wall
than the negative charge on the inside, thus the balloon sticks. This is due to the idea of \emph{conductors} and \emph{insulators}.

	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{balloon2.png}
	\caption{The negative balloon repels the wall's electrons, leaving a net positive surface. The balloon is then attracted to this induced positive charge.}
	\label{fig:example}
\end{figure}
Recall that a \textbf{conductor} is a type of material through which valence electrons (electrons in the outer shell) easily flows, and an \textbf{insulator} is a type of material through which charge does not easily flow, if at all. Metals, such as copper or zinc, are the best conductors because the electrons within the atoms are not tied to a nucleus, but rather form a sparse collection or sea in which the charge can flow through. Insulators like plastic, rubber, or glass are prevent charge flowing through as the electrons are tightly bound to their parent atoms or molecules.

\subsection{Point Charge}
The wall and the balloon all contain a bunch of tiny particles which we can refer to as \textbf{point charges}\index{point charge}. A point charge is an electric charge that is treated as a concentrated single point in space, with no physical size or volume. Similar to point masses, we use point charges to simulate effects of charge on other objects or points.

This charge acts parallel to the secant line that touches both point charges only
once.

\subsection{Superposition} %requires vector addition
What if there are more just one charges acting on a given point charge?
Coulomb's law gives the force between two point charges, but in a system with three
or more charges we apply the \textbf{principle of superposition}\index{principle
	of superposition}\footnote{Superposition is really a linear algebra topic, and not
	something that this textbook will cover, but it has so many real life
	applications and we recommend you do your own research! See the wiki page for more:
	\url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superposition_principle}}.

The principle of superposition states that the total electric force on a charge
is the vector sum of the individual forces exerted on it by all other charges.\footnote{This
	is similar how we calculate tensions when there are multiple tensions (or just forces
	in general) on an object. The net force is the sum of all the forces after vector
	addition is applied.}

\section{Electric Field}
\index{electric field}
Recall that, when we talked about Gravitational Fields, we defined them as fields of infinitesimal points that each have a magnitude of force associated with them. This field revolves around the entire 2D circle or 3D sphere (generally point masses, charges, fields, etc are spherical).

Similar to how the gravitational field extends out from the center of a given point mass, an \emph{electric field} of a point charge has (imaginary) vectors pointing out of it. However, the direction of the vectors\footnote{You may hear the terms electric field lines and electric field vectors used interchangibly} (and electric force) depends on the the charge of the point charge. A negatively charged point mass has force lines pointing \emph{towards} it, and a positively charged point mass has force lines pointing \emph{away} from it (\ref{fig:pos_ef}). In short, they always originate from positive charges and approach negative charges. %https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field

% Imagine that there is a charge $Q$ and a tiny particle test point $q_t$. Moving $q_t$, the test particle around, away from, further, and closer to the electric field of $Q$ changes the force that $Q$ feels. The force changes based on where the test particle $q_t$ is. Recall that force is a vector, and the electric field is also a vector, so we can create a resulting formula:
% \begin{equation}
% 	\vec{\mathbf{E}} = \frac{\vec{\mathbf{F}}_e}{q_t}
% \end{equation}

What are the \textit{units} of an electric field? Well, by this equation, $\vec{\mathbf{F}}_e$ is a force which we know is in \textit{Newtons} ($N$), and the test particle $q$ is in Coulombs ($C$). The resulting units are $N\,C^{-1}$, or Newton's per Coulomb\index{electric field!units}. Note that this is equivalent to $1\,V\,m^{-1}$, or one Volt per meter\footnote{Equivalently, one $kV\,cm^{-1}$, or kilovolt per centimeter is equal to $10^{-5} \times 1 N \,C^{-1}$. To reverse the operation, multiply the kilovolts per centimeter by $10^5$ to get the resulting Newtons per Coulomb}. This is how electric field forces are converted to volts within batteries or circuits!

It is important to note that we can simplify this this equation by substituting Coulomb's law:
\begin{equation}
	\vec{\mathbf{E}} = \frac{\vec{\mathbf{F}}_e}{q_t} = \frac{\frac{kq_1q_t}{r^2}}{q_t} = \frac{kQ}{r^2}
\end{equation}
where $Q$ is the electric field's source charge.

For positive charges $Q$, the force vectors get weaker the farther from the charge (visually, the force lines get shorter). In fact, it decreases by a factor of $\frac{1}{r^2}$. Think to yourself where this factor comes from. (Hint: refer back to Coulomb's law (\ref{eq:coulomb_law}))

\section{Electric Flux and Gauss's law}
\textbf{Electric Flux}\index{electric flux} is the number of field lines that pass through a given surface. Represented by $\Phi$, it is the measure of how much of an electric field itself passes through an area.

The electric flux depends on 3 factors:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Electric Field Strength ($\mathbf E$)
	\item Surface Area being measured, ($\mathbf A$). $\mathbf A$ must be perpendicular to the field lines.
	\item Angle at which the area is relative to the field lines.
\end{itemize}

If the electric field lines are perpendicular to the area $\mathbf A$, then the electric flux is
\begin{equation}
	\Phi = \sum \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{A}
	\label{eq:elec_flux_perp}
\end{equation}
Think of this as a paper directly perpendicular to a fan; the maximum amount of the paper is blown by the fan.

If the paper is at an angle to the fan, less flow from the fan affects the paper. If you tilt it until it's parallel to the wind, zero wind affects the paper. Similarly, the electric flux on a surface at a titled angle decreases how much flux is felt:
\begin{equation}
	\Phi = \sum \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{A} \cos(\theta)
	\label{eq:elec_flux_angle}
\end{equation}
In integral form (which we have not covered yet, so do not worry about using it), this is:
\begin{equation}
	\Phi = \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} \cos(\theta)
	\label{eq:elec_flux_integral}
\end{equation}
where the integral is on a closed area.

If you recall our discussion on resistance, flux and resistance are very similar measurements. In a wire, a larger cross-sectional area ($A$) is like a highway for electrons. It allows more electrons to pass through easily, which decreases the resistance. If you maximize flux by having a huge area, you are essentially creating a path with very low resistance for a current. 
\subsection{Gauss's law}
So far, we have imagined the electric field is only 2-dimensional plane (a circle). What if we consider them as a 3-dimensional boundary, similar to gravitation? This introduces the idea of \textit{Gauss's Law for Electric Fields}:
\begin{mdframed}[style=important, frametitle={Gauss's Law}]\index{Gauss's Law}
Gauss's law states that the strength of an electric field for 3-Dimensional object can be calculated by:
\begin{equation}
		\Phi = \oiint \vec{\textbf{E}} \ d\vec{\textbf{A}}\,\cos(\theta) = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0}
\end{equation}
This is a formula for saying that every infinitessimal bit of a 2-Dimensional object is given a height and an electric strength, and this is equal to the enclosed area of the charge over the permittivity of free space constant.
\end{mdframed}

For specifically spherically 3-Dimensional objects, inputting the formula for a sphere into $A$ gives us:
\begin{equation}
	E = \frac{q}{4\pi r^2\epsilon_0}
\end{equation}
where $q$ is the source charge, $r$ is the distance from that source charge, and $4\pi r^2$ is the surface area of the electric field. 
What this means is that electric field at all points $r$ from the source charge are equal in magnitude. Pretty cool!
\section{Continuous Charge Distributions}
In the previous sections, we treated charges as discrete ``dots'' in space. However, in applications of charges such as a long charged wire, a flat metal plate, or a solid plastic sphere, charge is spread out over a macroscopic object. When we have a collection of so many point charges that they appear to form a smooth, ``fluid-like'' surface or volume, we call it a \textbf{continuous charge distribution}\index{continuous charge distributions}. This means that the charges, while not truly continuous, can be considered continuous as a long single stream.

To analyze these, we stop summing individual particles ($\sum$) and instead use calculus to integrate ($\int$) over the entire object. While we have not covered calculus yet, keep in mind that this is just a way of calculating the area or volume of a continuous solid.
\subsection{Charge Densities}
The first step in any continuous distribution problem is defining how the charge is spread. We use three Greek letters to represent the "concentration" of charge based on the object's geometry:

        \textbf{Type} & \textbf{Symbol} & \textbf{Definition} & \textbf{SI Units} \\ 
		\hline
        Linear & $\lambda$ (lambda) & Charge per unit length & $\text{C/m}$ \\
        Surface & $\sigma$ (sigma) & Charge per unit area & $\text{C/m}^2$ \\

If the charge is distributed \textbf{uniformly}, these values stay constant. For example, for a rod of length $L$ and total charge $Q$, the linear density is simply $\lambda = Q/L$.
However, if these the charge is not constant per the unit of length, we must use calculus to find the total charge density. To find the total electric field $\vec{\mathbf{E}}$ at a point $P$ near a charged object, we imagine breaking the object into many infinitesimal "pieces" of charge, $dq$. Each chunk acts like a tiny point charge and contributes a tiny bit of electric field, $d\vec{\mathbf{E}}$.

Recall our point charge formula:
\begin{equation}
    d\vec{\mathbf{E}} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{dq}{r^2}
\end{equation}

To find the total field, we integrate over the entire distribution:
\begin{equation}
    \vec{\mathbf{E}} = \int d\vec{\mathbf{E}} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \int \frac{dq}{r^2}
\end{equation}
The key to solving these integrals is replacing $dq$ with the appropriate density:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Lines: $dq = \lambda \, dl$
    \item Surfaces: $dq = \sigma \, dA$
    \item Volumes: $dq = \rho \, dV$
\end{itemize}

For now, you will not be quizzed on these integrals but know of their existence and that charge can be treated as a virtual stream along an object. This is why any part of the balloon from our first example will stick to any part of the wall.

\section{Electric Potential}
Let's talk about electric potential and electric potential energy. Recall that objects can have \textit{kinetic} and \textit{potential} energy, that potential energy is gradually transported into kinetic energy. 

Electric Potential describes the amount of work, $W_E$, done by an electric force. The change in \textbf{electric potential energy}\index{electric potential energy} is defined as the work done on that force:
\begin{equation}
	\Delta U_E = -W_E \label{eq:change_in_epe}
\end{equation}

Notice how this is similar to an object of mass $m$ undergoing work done by a gravitational field $\Delta U_G = -W_G$.

In both cases, the field \textit{tries} to push the object toward a state of lower potential energy. A positive charge naturally wants to move with the electric field toward lower potential; in this case, its electric potential energy is converted into kinetic energy. Conversely, to move a positive charge against an electric field, work must be done on the charge, causing its electric potential to increase.

\subsection{Point Charges, Uniform Fields, and Spherical Shells}
In a \textbf{uniform electric field} (such as the space between two large parallel plates), the field strength $E$ is constant. The potential difference between two points separated by a distance $d$ along the field lines is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta V = -Ed
\end{equation}
For a \textbf{point charge} $Q$, we typically set the reference point ($V=0$) at infinity. The potential at a distance $r$ from the charge is:
\begin{equation}
    V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r}
	\label{eq:point_charge}
\end{equation}
Unlike the electric field, which is a vector and follows an $1/r^2$ factor, electric potential is a \textbf{scalar} and follows an $1/r$ factor.
\subsection*{Surface Spherical Charge}
For all spherical objects of radius $R$ (surface spheres - assume hollow interior), it has potential inside and outside of the shell. For all points outside the shell, it is the same for any point charge $Q$, as seen in equation~\ref{eq:point_charge}. For all points \emph{inside} the shell, the potential is
$$V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{R}$$
This means that moving a test charge $q$ from the surface of the shell to the center of the shell, the potential does not change. The potential is constant within the shell.

When the sphere is \emph{not hollow}, the interior charge changes to:
$$k = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{Q}{2R} \left(3 - \frac{r^2}{R^2}\right)$$

\subsection{Potential due to Configurations of Charge}
Because potential is a scalar, finding the total potential from multiple charges is often simpler than finding the total electric field. We do not need to worry about components, instead, we simply sum the potentials:
\begin{equation}
    V = \sum_i \frac{k q_i}{r_i} \quad \text{or for continuous distributions} \quad V = k \int \frac{dq}{r}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Conservation of Energy}
When a charge $q$ moves through a potential difference $\Delta V$, the change in its electric potential energy is $\Delta U_E = q\Delta V$. If no other forces are acting on the charge, the total mechanical energy is conserved:
\begin{equation}
    K_a + U_{E,a} = K_b + U_{E,b} \implies \frac{1}{2}mv_a^2 + qV_a = \frac{1}{2}mv_b^2 + qV_b
\end{equation}

\section{Lightning}\index{lightning}

A great deal of lightning moves within a cloud or between clouds. However, sometimes
it jumps to the earth. These bolts of lightning vary in the amount of electrons
they carry, but the average is about 15 coulombs.

in the center, right?''

We aren't ready to talk about this either, but quantum mechanics tells us that electrons
like to live in a certain specific energy level. Hugging protons isn't one of
those levels.
