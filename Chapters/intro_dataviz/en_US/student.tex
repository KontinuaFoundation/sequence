\chapter{Introduction to Data Visualization}
% (or Graphical Methods for Describing Data)

It is difficult for the human mind to look at a list of numbers and
identify the patterns in them, so we often use these numbers to make a picture. These pictures are called \textit{graphs},
\textit{charts}, or \textit{plots}. Often, the right picture can make
the meaning in the data obvious. \textbf{Data visualization} is the process of making pictures from numbers.

\section{Types of Variables}

Depending on the type of data and what you are trying to demonstrate
about it, you will use different types of data visualizations. Before we get to the more complex types of data visualizations, let's
look at the two types of variables we are usually looking to visualize: \textit{categorical} and \textit{numerical}.

\textit{Categorical} or qualitative variables are those that can be put into categories. For example, the type of shoes a person wears 
(sneakers, sandals, etc.) is a categorical variable. The type of shoes sold is a categorical variable. The name of the salesperson (John, Jane, etc.) is also a categorical variable.

\textit{Numerical} or quantitative variables are those that can be measured with numbers. For example, the number of boxes of cookies 
sold is a quantitative variable. The average temperature on a given day is also a quantitative variable. Quantitative variables can be 
further divided into \textit{discrete} and \textit{continuous} variables. Discrete variables are those that can only take on specific values, 
such as the number of pencils sold (you can't sell 3.5 pencils). Continuous variables can take on any value within a range, 
such as the average height of students in a class (which can be 5.5 feet, 1.65 m, etc.).

Further, we can also classify data into univariate, bivariate, and multivariate data. Univariate data has only one variable, 
such as the number of boxes of cookies sold. Bivariate data has two variables, such as the average temperature and total sales 
for a lemonade stand. Multivariate data has more than two variables, such as the average temperature, total sales, and type of 
ookie sold. We will only look at univariate and bivariate data!

\section{How do we describe data?}

% Insert blurb about this.

There are different descriptive methods for representing data, and they can usually be grouped into three categories: 
\textit{tabular}, \textit{graphical}, and \textit{numerical}.

\textit{Tabular} methods include tables, which are a way of organizing data into rows and columns. 
They are useful for displaying raw data and making it easy to look up specific values. However, 
they can be difficult to interpret when dealing with large datasets or when trying to identify patterns.

\textit{Graphical} methods include bar charts, line graphs, pie charts, and scatter plots.
They are useful for visualizing data and making it easier to identify patterns and trends.

\textit{Numerical} methods include measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode) and measures of variability (range, variance, standard deviation).
They are useful for summarizing data and providing a numerical representation of the data's characteristics. 

We will focus on graphical methods in this chapter, but we will also touch on tabular and numerical methods in later chapters.

\subsection{Dotplot}

A \textit{dotplot} is one of the simplest ways to display single, quantitaive variables.
Each observation is represented by a dot placed above its value on a number line.
If a value appears more than once, the dots stack vertically.

Dotplots are most effective for small to moderate data sets. If the data set is too large,
a dotplot can become cluttered and hard to read. In that case, a histogram or a boxplot may be a better choice.\index{dotplot}

\textbf{When to Use a Dotplot}
\begin{itemize}
    \item You have 1 variable.
    \item The variable is quantitative.
    \item You want to see the distribution and the individual data values.
    \item The data set is not too large.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{How to Make a Dotplot}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Draw a horizontal number line (x-axis) that covers the data range.
    \item Choose a scale that fits the entire range of values.
    \item Place one dot above the location of each observation.
    \item If multiple observations have the same value, stack the dots vertically.
\end{enumerate}

Here is an example of a dotplot.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering

    \label{fig:dotplotExample}

\textbf{How to Read a Dotplot}

Each dot shows the location of one data value. To find the value of a dot,
look straight down to the number line.

A dotplot helps you describe the distribution by answering questions like:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Spread: How spread out are the values (range and clustering)?
    \item Shape: Do the dots form a roughly symmetric shape, or is the distribution skewed?
    \item Center: Where is the distribution “balanced” (a typical value)?
    \item Outliers: Are there values far away from the rest?\index{outlier}
\end{itemize}

Sometimes a dotplot shows a distribution with a long tail to one side.
For example, salary data often has many values in the lower-to-middle range
and a few extremely high values. This creates a right-skewed distribution.

Outliers can strongly affect numerical summaries, especially the mean.

\textbf{Connecting Outliers and the Mean (Seesaw Idea)}
A useful way to think about the mean is as a balancing point.
Imagine the dotplot as a seesaw: points far from the main cluster have more “pull.”
So a single extreme value can shift the mean noticeably, even if most data values stay the same.

\textbf{Comparative Dotplots}\index{comparative dotplot}

A \textit{comparative dotplot} uses the same numerical scale to compare
two (or more) groups. This is useful when you want to compare distributions side-by-side.

When comparing groups, focus on differences in:
\begin{itemize}
    \item center (typical value),
    \item spread,
    \item shape (skew/clusters),
    \item and outliers.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Make a Dotplot in a Spreadsheet} 

You can create a dotplot in Excel or Google Sheets using a scatter plot and a helper column.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Put your data values in column A (starting in A2). Sort them (recommended).
    \item In cell B2, enter:
    
    \texttt{=COUNTIF(\$A\$2:A2, A2)}
    
    Fill down. This creates stacking heights for repeated x-values.
    \item Insert a Scatter chart using column A as x-values and column B as y-values.
    \item Format the chart:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item markers only (no lines),
        \item hide the y-axis labels (y is only for stacking),
        \item add titles and adjust the x-axis scale.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Stemplot}

A \textit{stemplot} (also called a \textit{stem-and-leaf plot}) is an effective way to summarize
\textbf{one quantitative variable} when the data set is not too large. Like a dotplot, it helps you see the
overall distribution, but a stemplot has an extra advantage: it still shows the exact data values.

To make a stemplot, each observation is split into two parts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the stem, which is the left-most part of the number (leading digit(s))
    \item the leaf, which is the remaining part, usually the final digit
\end{itemize}

For example, $23$ can be written as $2 \mid 3$.

\textbf{How to Make a Stemplot}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Choose stems and leaves. Decide where to split each number into a stem and a leaf.
    There is no single rule for this. Your goal is to choose a reasonable number of stems.
    \item Draw a vertical divider. Stems go on the left; leaves go on the right.
    \item List all stems in order. Make sure the stems cover the full range of your data.
    \item Write leaves for each observation. Place each leaf next to its correct stem.
    \item Sort leaves. Put the leaves for each stem in increasing order.
    \item Include a key. The key shows how to interpret the stems and leaves.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Choosing the Number of Stems}

If you use \textit{too few stems}, you can hide patterns because the data get lumped together.
If you use \textit{too many stems}, you can also hide patterns because the plot becomes too spread out.

A common strategy is to start with stems that represent groups of 10 (tens digits as stems),
and then adjust if needed.

Sometimes a single stem represents too wide of an interval. In that case, you can split stems
by repeating each stem twice:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the first row holds leaves $0$--$4$
    \item the second row holds leaves $5$--$9$
\end{itemize}

For example, if your data run from 10 to 49, you can use stems
\[
1, 1, 2, 2, 3, 3, 4, 4
\]
where the first $1$ represents 10--14 and the second $1$ represents 15--19, and so on.

\textbf{Example}

Suppose these are housing costs (in dollars per month):
\[
255, 259, 304, 306, 309, 317, 323, 327, 332, 334, 342, 349, 350, 351, 362, 367, 376, 384, 401, 403
\]

If we use the hundreds digit as the stem and the last two digits as the leaf, we can split each stem into two rows:
(top row for leaves 00--49, bottom row for leaves 50--99)

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c | l}
Stem & Leaves \\
\hline
2 & 55\ 59 \\
2 &  \\
3 & 04\ 06\ 09\ 17\ 23\ 27\ 32\ 34\ 42\ 49 \\
3 & 50\ 51\ 62\ 67\ 76\ 84 \\
4 & 01\ 03 \\
4 &  \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Key:} $3 \mid 04 = 304$ dollars per month

\textbf{How to Read a Stemplot}

A stemplot gives you several kinds of information at once:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Exact values: Each leaf represents a single observation.
    \item Frequencies: The number of leaves in a row tells how many observations fall in that interval.
    For example, if a stem row has 9 leaves, that means 9 observations are in that range.
    \item Gaps: If a stem has no leaves, that means there are no observations in that interval.
    \item Shape and spread: You can see whether the values cluster in certain ranges and whether the distribution is skewed.
    \item Center: You can estimate a typical value by locating where the leaves cluster most.
    \item Outliers: Unusually high or low values may stand out because they appear far from the rest.
\end{itemize}

Just like with dotplots, you can imagine the distribution as something that could ``balance'' on a finger:
the place it would balance is a rough sense of the center.

\subsection{Histogram}

A \textit{histogram} is one of the most popular ways to display single quantitaive variables.
It is especially useful for showing patterns in larger data sets, where dotplots or stemplots can become
overwhelming. A histogram is like a stemplot turned on its side: instead of listing individual values, we group values
into intervals called classes or bins, and draw a bar for each bin.

Histograms are best when you care about the overall shape of a distribution (clusters, skew, gaps, outliers),
not the exact value of every data point.

A histogram can be drawn using frequency (counts), relative frequency (proportions), and percent frequency (percentages). These three versions have the same shape if they use the same bins. The difference is only the y-axis scale.

\textbf{How to Make a Histogram}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Create groups from continuous data. This process is called binning.
    \item Draw the x-axis and y-axis, and scale them to fit the class intervals and the frequencies
    (or relative frequencies or percentages).
    \item Draw one bar for each class. The height of each bar equals the frequency (or relative frequency or percent)
    for that class.
    \item Draw the bars touching with no gaps (because the data values are continuous and the bins connect).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{How to Read a Histogram}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Each bar represents a single class (bin). There is one bar per class.
    \item The bins are placed on the x-axis in increasing order, like a number line.
    \item The height of a bar tells how many observations fall in that class (for a frequency histogram).
    \item A class with no bar means no observations fall in that interval (a gap).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Example of Interpreting Bars}

Suppose a histogram uses the bins \$250--\$300, \$300--\$350, \$350--\$400, and so on.
\begin{itemize}
    \item If the first bar (250--300) has height 9, then 9 observations are between \$250 and \$300.
    \item If the second and third bars (300--350 and 350--400) have heights 14 and 20,
    then $14 + 20 = 34$ observations are between \$300 and \$400.
    \item If there is no bar for 700--750, then there are 0 observations between \$700 and \$750.
    \item If the bars beyond 700 have heights 0, 1, and 1, then $0+1+1=2$ observations are at least \$700.
\end{itemize}


A histogram can help you to describe the distribution by focusing on shape, center, spread, gaps, and outliers.

\textbf{Making a Histogram in a Spreadsheet}

You can make a histogram in Excel or Google Sheets.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Put the data in one column.
    \item Choose bin widths (for example, bins of width 50: 250--300, 300--350, 350--400, etc.).
    \item Insert a histogram chart (or use a column chart after building a frequency table).
    \item Check the bin width and the endpoints. Changing the bins can change the appearance of the distribution.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{How do we compare dotplots, stemplots, and histograms?}

For small data sets, dotplots and stemplots can be better because they show the exact values.
In a histogram, you lose that detail because values are grouped into bins. In exchange, histograms make it easier
to see the overall pattern in large data sets.

\subsection{Cumulative Frequency Plot}

A \textit{cumulative frequency plot} (also called a \textit{cumulative frequency chart})
shows the running total of how many data values are at or below each value (or at or below each class boundary).
Instead of showing how many observations fall in each bin like a histogram, a cumulative frequency plot shows
how the totals add up as you move from left to right.

Cumulative frequency plots are useful when you want to answer questions like:
\begin{itemize}
    \item How many observations are less than or equal to a certain value?
    \item How many observations are greater than or equal to a certain value?
    \item What percent of the data fall below a certain cutoff?
\end{itemize}

\textbf{How to Make a Cumulative Frequency Plot}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Draw the x-axis and y-axis.
    \item Set up bins for the data and identify the upper class boundaries (the right endpoints).
    \item Compute the cumulative frequency at each upper class boundary.
    
    (This means: add the frequency of that class to the total from all previous bins.)
    \item Plot a point at each upper class boundary with height equal to the cumulative frequency.
    \item Connect the points with straight line segments.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Interpreting the shape of your plot}

The steepness of the curve tells you where the data values are concentrated.
\begin{itemize}
    \item If the curve rises quickly over a certain x-interval, many observations fall in that range.
    \item If the curve is almost flat, few observations fall in that range.
\end{itemize}

A cumulative frequency plot can also hint at skewness:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Right-skewed:} the curve increases quickly at smaller values, then levels off later.
    \item \textbf{Left-skewed:} the curve increases slowly at first, then rises sharply near the end.
    \item \textbf{Symmetric:} the curve often looks S-shaped.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Example}

Suppose $n=80$ observations were grouped into bins, and the cumulative frequency at $x = 350$ is about 23.
Then:
\begin{itemize}
    \item About 23 observations are $\le 350$.
    \item About $80 - 23 = 57$ observations are $\ge 350$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Making a Cumulative Frequency Plot in Python}

This example shows the basic idea: build bins, count how many values fall in each bin, then take a cumulative sum.

\begin{minted}{python}
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Replace with your data
data = np.array([250, 275, 310, 320, 350, 365, 410, 415, 430, 500, 525, 610, 720, 840])

# Choose bin edges (upper class boundaries are the right endpoints)
bins = np.arange(250, 851, 50)  # 250, 300, ..., 850

# Frequency in each bin
counts, edges = np.histogram(data, bins=bins)

# Cumulative frequency at each upper boundary
cumulative_counts = np.cumsum(counts)
upper_bounds = edges[1:]  # right endpoints

plt.plot(upper_bounds, cumulative_counts, marker='o')
plt.title("Cumulative Frequency Plot")
plt.xlabel("Upper class boundary ($)")
plt.ylabel("Cumulative frequency")
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()
\end{minted}

If you change the \texttt{bin} width, the curve may look different, but it will always increase from left to right and end at $n$.
